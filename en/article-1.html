THE FORGOTTEN STAR OF CAMBRIDGE

WRITTEN BY Deniz Ozer, October 21, 2025

(UPDATED October 22, 2025)

In 1967, a young doctoral student discovered a strange signal from the sky. What she didn't know yet was that she had just revolutionized radioastronomy. Yet her name would remain in the shadows for a long time. An emblematic story of the Matilda Effect.

Jocelyn Bell Burnell was only twenty-four years old when she made one of the greatest discoveries of the 20th century. A PhD student at Cambridge University, she was participating in the construction of a gigantic radio telescope designed to study quasars. The days were tough: cables to lay, measurements to verify, data to decipher.

Among the kilometers of recorded graphs, she noticed a repetitive pattern, a regular beat coming from deep space. Intrigued, she noted the position, compared, verified. The signal returned, always identical. It wasn't noise or interference. It was something.

At first, no one really believed her. The signal was too precise to be natural. As a joke, her colleagues called it "LGM-1", a humorous acronym for Little Green Men. But Bell Burnell persisted. After weeks of verification, she identified several similar sources. She had discovered pulsars, those rapidly rotating neutron stars that emit periodic radio waves.

Astronomy had just opened up to a new territory: that of celestial rhythms.

Seven years later, the 1974 Nobel Prize in Physics rewarded this discovery... but not Jocelyn Bell Burnell. The prize was awarded to her thesis supervisor, Antony Hewish, and to Martin Ryle. The young woman was absent from the list. The scientific world applauded, but one voice was missing. This silence is that of the Matilda Effect.

Bell Burnell doesn't claim the glory. She humbly stated: "A student doesn't receive the Nobel Prize in Physics." Yet her calm attitude doesn't erase the injustice. Her case would become a global symbol of stolen recognition, a perfect mirror of the gender bias that runs through the history of science.

Decades later, the scientific community tried to repair the omission. In 2018, she received the Breakthrough Prize in Fundamental Physics, worth three million dollars. True to her values, she donated the entire sum to fund scholarships for women, minorities, and refugees in science.

Jocelyn Bell Burnell's story illustrates a simple truth: scientific genius is not enough. You also need to be heard. And in a world where science is still marked by reflexes of exclusion, Bell Burnell's voice resonates like a pulsar - a persistent, regular signal, impossible to silence.
