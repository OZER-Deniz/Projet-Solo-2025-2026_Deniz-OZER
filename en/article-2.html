WHEN HISTORY FORGETS WOMEN IN SCIENCE

WRITTEN BY Deniz Ozer, on October 21, 2025

(UPDATED on October 22, 2025)

How many discoveries attributed to "great men" are actually the work of forgotten female researchers? From Jocelyn Bell Burnell to Rosalind Franklin, the Matilda Effect puts a name to a centuries-old mechanism: the systematic invisibilization of women scientists.

The history of science has long been written by men and, too often, for men. Behind the great names that adorn textbooks, countless women have contributed to major discoveries without ever receiving the credit they deserved.

This phenomenon has a name: the Matilda Effect, theorized in 1993 by historian Margaret W. Rossiter. It pays tribute to Matilda Joslyn Gage, a 19th-century feminist activist who already denounced the erasure of women from intellectual history.

The Matilda Effect is based on an insidious mechanism: when women make a discovery, it is often attributed to their male colleagues, or even ignored. Examples abound:

  • Rosalind Franklin, whose diffraction images allowed Watson and Crick to model the double helix of DNA.
  • Lise Meitner, co-discoverer of nuclear fission, excluded from the Nobel Prize in favor of Otto Hahn.
  • Jocelyn Bell Burnell, of course, erased from the reward for the discovery of pulsars.

What do they have in common? They all worked in a system where masculine scientific authority was the norm.

But the Matilda Effect is not limited to isolated cases. It extends to structural mechanisms: underrepresentation in publications, bias in evaluation committees, lack of female role models for subsequent generations. In the academic world, women remain a minority in so-called "hard" disciplines such as physics, engineering, or computer science.

To fight against this invisibilization, recent movements, such as #NoMoreMatildas, encourage young people to rediscover these forgotten figures. The project "The Matilda Effect - Jocelyn Bell Burnell" is part of this approach: making the invisible visible, not out of compassion, but out of recognition. Because the issue is not only to tell the story of women, but to rewrite that of science in its complete truth.

The Matilda Effect acts as a mirror: it reveals how much science is shaped by society. And recognizing this bias is already beginning to repair it.
