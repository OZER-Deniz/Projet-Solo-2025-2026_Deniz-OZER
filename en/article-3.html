STELLAR HEARTBEATS AT THE HEART OF THE UNIVERSE

WRITTEN BY Deniz Ozer, on October 21, 2025

(UPDATED on October 22, 2025)

Pulsars are cosmic lighthouses. Born from the death of massive stars, they beat like stellar hearts, sending their signals across the universe. Their discovery, made by Jocelyn Bell Burnell, was a triumph... that history first forgot.

Pulsars are fascinating objects: tiny, dense, and stunningly precise. They are born from the violent end of a massive star, which explodes in a supernova before collapsing in on itself. What remains is a neutron star, a core about ten kilometers in diameter, but a trillion times denser than steel.

When these stars spin rapidly on their axis, their magnetic fields concentrate radio emissions into narrow beams. If Earth is in the path of these beams, we perceive a periodic signal - a regular beat that can last for billions of years.

In 1967, it was this rhythm that Jocelyn Bell Burnell detected. At the time, the nature of this signal was not yet understood. Some saw it as an extraterrestrial message. But Bell Burnell and her team quickly confirmed that it was a natural astrophysical phenomenon.

Pulsars then became valuable tools for physics:

  • They allow us to test Einstein's general relativity.
  • They serve as cosmic clocks, measuring time with unmatched precision.
  • They help map the Milky Way and detect gravitational waves.

Ironically, the discovery of these "beating" stars also reflects a social beat: that of a scientific world where the name of the woman discoverer was erased. The constant rhythm of the pulsar thus becomes a metaphor for persistence - that of truth, which always ends up being heard.
