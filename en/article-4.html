SCIENCE MEETS FICTION

WRITTEN BY Deniz Ozer, on October 21, 2025

(UPDATED on October 22, 2025)

When Stanley Kubrick imagines 2001: A Space Odyssey, humanity is about to walk on the Moon. But beyond space travel, the film questions our relationship to knowledge, creation, and memory. A year earlier, Jocelyn Bell Burnell discovered pulsars - a real echo of Kubrick's fiction, and another story of erasure.

1968. America lives to the rhythm of the space race. The world looks to the sky, rockets take off, and Stanley Kubrick, a perfectionist filmmaker, is about to project humanity into infinity. His film, 2001: A Space Odyssey, disrupts everything: no heroes, no useless dialogue, no triumphant music. Instead, hypnotic images, a ballet of machines, cosmic silence. Kubrick wants to show what science feels, not just what it sees.

But what few viewers know is that as he films these sequences, a young Irish researcher, Jocelyn Bell Burnell, picks up a mysterious signal in a field of radio telescopes: a star beat at regular intervals. She has just discovered pulsars, these neutron stars that spin on themselves like cosmic beacons.

Two stories, two looks at the sky - one fictional, the other very real - united by the same fascination for the invisible.

In 2001, Kubrick explores the great themes of humanity: artificial intelligence, evolution, the loneliness of the researcher facing the unknown. Bell Burnell, on the other hand, lives these questions in the reality of the laboratory. Like Bowman's character facing HAL 9000, she struggles against doubt, misunderstanding, and the skeptical gaze of others. The signal she observes doesn't "fit" any known model, and her perseverance will earn her a major discovery... then a symbolic exclusion: the 1974 Nobel Prize in Physics eludes her.

This parallel between fiction and reality illuminates the Matilda Effect from a new angle. Kubrick, in 1968, imagined a future where man dialogues with the stars - but his film remains, like its era, entirely masculine. Not a single woman scientist aboard Discovery One. The future, even dreamed, was still thought by and for men. Similarly, Bell Burnell's real world does not yet fully grant women their place in research.

Yet the spirit of the film deeply aligns with Bell Burnell's struggle: it celebrates curiosity, the quest for meaning, the beauty of knowledge. Both remind us that understanding the universe is not an act of domination, but an act of humility. Kubrick shows humans facing the immensity of the cosmos; Bell Burnell captures the tenuous voice of stars. Two ways of looking at the sky, with the same emotion.

Today, rereading 2001 in light of the Matilda Effect means questioning the way science and its cultural representation have long erased half of humanity. If Kubrick's odyssey paid tribute to artificial intelligence, Bell Burnell's odyssey pays tribute to human intelligence, patient and silent. And in both cases, the message remains the same: in space or in science, curiosity has no gender.
