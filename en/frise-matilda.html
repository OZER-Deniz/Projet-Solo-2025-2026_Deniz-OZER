OUR MATILDA TIMELINE

THESE WOMEN WHO MADE SCIENCE IN THE SHADOWS

The history of science is not only that of discoveries, but also that of silences. Behind each great advancement often hide researchers, thinkers, explorers whose names have been erased, minimized, or attributed to others.

This phenomenon has a name: the Matilda Effect, in tribute to Matilda Joslyn Gage, the activist who, as early as the 19th century, denounced the invisibilization of women in knowledge.

This timeline traces the journey of these forgotten or belatedly recognized women, from Antiquity to the present day. They observed the stars, deciphered matter, revealed life, but their genius was often eclipsed.

Today, their stories reappear, like constellations long lost in the night of time.

From Hypatia to Jocelyn Bell Burnell, from Rosalind Franklin to Vera Rubin, these Matildas remind us of an essential truth:

"science truly progresses only when it gives voice to all those who make it."

Chronological timeline of Matildas

ÉMILIE DU CHÂTELET

(1706-1749)

Field: Physics and philosophy

Translated and commented on Newton's Principia Mathematica in French, making Newtonian physics accessible to Europe. Her work is attributed to Voltaire or minimized as mere "accompanying notes".

"Without her, France would never have read Newton."

LISE MEITNER

(1878-1968)

Field: Nuclear physics

Co-discovered nuclear fission with Otto Hahn in 1938. Otto Hahn alone received the 1944 Nobel Prize in Chemistry. Meitner, though responsible for the theoretical interpretation, was ignored.

"She explained the bomb... but refused to build it."

ROSALIND FRANKLIN

(1920-1958)

Field: Biophysics, molecular biology

Thanks to her X-ray diffraction images, she enabled Watson and Crick to discover the double helix structure of DNA. Died before official recognition; her role was fully acknowledged only after her death.

"She revealed the code of life, without ever being cited."

JOCELYN BELL BURNELL

(BORN 1943)

Field: Astrophysics, radio astronomy

Discovered pulsars in 1967, rapidly rotating neutron stars. Her thesis supervisor received the 1974 Nobel Prize in Physics; she was excluded from the reward.

"She heard the heartbeat of the universe - but her name was silenced."

VERA RUBIN

(1928-2016)

Field: Astronomy

Discovered observational evidence of dark matter, revolutionizing our understanding of the universe. Long excluded from the Nobel despite a major discovery.

"She saw what no one wanted to see: the invisible universe."

CHIEN-SHIUNG WU

(1912-1997)

Field: Experimental physics

Experimentally confirmed parity violation in beta decay, revolutionizing particle physics. Her male colleagues, Lee and Yang, received the 1957 Nobel Prize; she did not.

"Madame Wu did the test, but not the history."

HENRIETTA LEAVITT

(1868-1921)

Field: Astronomy

Discovered the relationship between luminosity and period of Cepheids, allowing measurement of distances in the universe. Her discovery served as the basis for Hubble, but her name remained absent from textbooks for decades.

"Without her, Hubble's universe would have had no scale."

NETTIE STEVENS

(1861-1912)

Field: Genetics

Identified the X and Y sex chromosomes. Discovery attributed to her colleague Thomas Hunt Morgan, who would receive the Nobel Prize.

"She discovered the letters of sex, but they erased her from the dictionary."

HEATHER COUPER

(1949-2020)

Field: Astrophysics, science communication

Author of The Astronomy Bible, pioneer in British science communication. Often perceived as "presenter" rather than scientist, despite her training and work.

"She made the stars shine at eye level."
