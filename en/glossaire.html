GLOSSARY

Pulsar

A rapidly rotating neutron star emitting regular electromagnetic radiation in the form of beams. Pulsars were discovered by Jocelyn Bell Burnell in 1967 and revolutionized our understanding of the universe. Their rotation period is extremely precise, comparable to that of an atomic clock.

Matilda Effect

Systematic phenomenon of invisibilization, minimization, or erroneous attribution of women's scientific contributions to their male colleagues. Named in tribute to Matilda Joslyn Gage who denounced these mechanisms in the 19th century. This bias affects the historical and contemporary recognition of women scientists.

Radio Astronomy

Branch of astronomy that studies celestial objects by observing their radio emissions. This discipline has enabled many major discoveries including pulsars, quasars, and cosmic background radiation.

Neutron Star

Ultra-dense remnant of a massive star after its collapse in a supernova. With a diameter of about 20 km, it contains more mass than the Sun. Its density is so extreme that a teaspoon of its matter would weigh several billion tons.

Nuclear Fission

Process of splitting a heavy atomic nucleus into two lighter nuclei, releasing a large amount of energy. This major scientific discovery is theoretically explained by Lise Meitner in 1938, although the Nobel Prize was awarded only to her colleague Otto Hahn.

X-ray Diffraction

Crystallographic analysis technique using X-rays to determine the three-dimensional structure of molecules. Rosalind Franklin mastered this method and produced the famous "Photo 51" which revealed the double helix structure of DNA.

DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid)

Molecule carrying genetic information in all living beings. Its double helix structure was discovered thanks to the work of Rosalind Franklin, although Watson and Crick alone received the initial credit for this fundamental discovery.

Dark Matter

Form of invisible matter that does not react with light, comprising about 85% of the total matter in the universe. Its existence is demonstrated by Vera Rubin's observations on galaxy rotation in the 1970s, revolutionizing modern cosmology.

Galactic Rotation Curve

Graph representing the rotational velocity of stars as a function of their distance from the galactic center. Vera Rubin discovered that these curves remain flat contrary to predictions, proving the existence of invisible matter: dark matter.

Parity (Physics)

Fundamental symmetry principle in quantum physics according to which the laws of nature remain identical in a mirror. Chien-Shiung Wu experimentally demonstrated in 1956 that this symmetry is violated in beta decay, revolutionizing particle physics.

Beta Radioactivity

Type of radioactive decay where an atomic nucleus emits an electron or positron. Wu's experiment on beta decay of cobalt-60 at ultra-low temperature proves parity violation, confirming Lee and Yang's theory.

Nobel Prize

International distinction rewarding exceptional contributions in physics, chemistry, medicine, literature, and peace. Nobel history particularly illustrates the Matilda effect: many women scientists have seen their discoveries rewarded to their male colleagues.

Supernova

Cataclysmic explosion marking the end of life of a massive star. This phenomenon releases in a few days as much energy as the Sun produces during its entire life. Supernovae create and disperse heavy chemical elements in the universe.

Quasar

Extremely luminous galactic nucleus powered by a supermassive black hole. Quasars are among the brightest and most distant objects in the observable universe. Their study reveals the evolution of primitive galaxies.

Electromagnetic Spectrum

Set of all electromagnetic waves classified by wavelength or frequency, from radio waves to gamma rays. The study of different portions of the spectrum reveals distinct aspects of celestial objects, enriching our understanding of the universe.
